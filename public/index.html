Project 01 · Fintech / Mortgage · AI Product · BPO Operations

From compliance crisis to growth engine — in 60 days

Helport AI sells an AI-powered call assistant to sales teams — it listens to live calls and surfaces real-time guidance so agents can respond to complex questions without training. I was brought in to deploy this for a mortgage company's BPO sales floor. What I inherited: a compliance blocker nobody wanted to solve, a skeptical client, and zero operational infrastructure.

The background

Helport AI's product was working. For three months, AEs were using the AI call assistant in real sales conversations — real-time guidance, post-call summaries, Q&A support. Adoption was normal. Nobody was talking about compliance.

Then an industry-wide event changed everything: the first known case of a company being fined for using AI on calls without disclosure. In the US — especially California — recording or AI-assisted calls require both parties to be informed. Overnight, compliance became a real concern.

The real problem

The legal requirement was clear. The harder problem was human: AEs refused to adapt their behavior. Adding a disclosure line — "this call is being recorded and assisted by AI" — felt unnatural, broke sales flow, made them sound less confident, and they believed it would hurt conversion rates.

This wasn't a technical failure or a policy gap. It was behavioral resistance at the intersection of legal requirement, user psychology, and business risk — and it nearly killed the project.

We tried to engineer around it. We proposed cloning each AE's voice to auto-insert the disclosure, invisible to the caller. We built a QA layer that would silently stop recording the moment disclosure wasn't detected — keeping the call alive, staying compliant. Both solutions were technically sound. The client declined both.

Because the real blocker was never the words. AEs didn't want callers to know AI was in the room — full stop. No amount of clever engineering solves a problem rooted in ego and sales identity. The target user had to change. AE pushback escalated to a collective refusal — and the project came close to being shut down entirely.

Key decisions
01Diagnosed the real blocker — and stopped trying to fix the wrong thing
Everyone was treating this as a compliance problem to solve at the product level. I reframed it: the product was fine. The problem was that AEs, as relationship-driven salespeople, would never comfortably say a disclosure line on a warm call. No amount of product tweaking would fix a behavior rooted in sales psychology. The target user had to change.
02Pivoted to call center agents — where disclosure is natural
Call center agents work from scripts. Their callers don't expect a warm relationship — they expect a process. Adding a disclosure line to the top of a script is completely natural in this context. The same compliance requirement that paralyzed AEs was a non-issue for agents. I rebuilt the entire deployment around this environment: new recruiting, new scripts, new training, CRM, dashboards, SOPs — all designed for a scripted, managed call center operation.
03Used agent data to create internal competitive pressure
Once agents were running with AI assistance, their performance data became a mirror held up to the AE team. Agents — lower cost, fully scripted — were producing comparable results. This gave the client's management a concrete, data-backed way to evaluate their AE team's output, tighten accountability, and make staffing decisions. The AI rollout became an internal performance tool, not just a sales tool.
04Built a shared dashboard — before anyone asked
The client had no visibility into their own loan pipeline. I proposed a shared dashboard — accessible to both sides, tamper-proof — tracking the full funnel: transfers and non-transfers with reasons → broker sign-up → loan scenario → submitted → denied / approved → closed loan. The logic was simple: we deliver the leads, but a lead is only as good as what happens next. This dashboard made sure AEs couldn't let good leads go cold. If a transfer didn't convert, we could see exactly where it dropped and why — whether that was our lead quality or their follow-through. No more finger-pointing. No more leads going to waste.

Project 02 · AI Mobile App · 0 → 1 Product · iOS

Helport Go — AI-powered mobile CRM, shipped in 2.5 months

Helport AI had a desktop product for real estate agents — an AI assistant that listened to calls and gave real-time guidance. The product existed. But almost nobody was actually using it in the field. I went to find out why.

The discovery

I asked a client to take me along on a real showing. What I saw: realtors and buyers meet inside the property, walking room to room during the inspection, talking the whole time. Nobody opens a laptop in someone's living room. They use their phones — to take notes, share listings, answer questions on the spot.

The desktop product wasn't just inconvenient. It was physically unusable in the context that mattered most. I came back and ran a survey through our sales team's network: 90% of realtors used only their phones for client work. The pivot wasn't optional — it was overdue.

Key decisions
01Said no to the boss's feature — with data
Leadership wanted to add a virtual meeting bot that would auto-join and record online meetings — like an Otter AI clone. The survey showed only 1 in 20 realtors ever held virtual meetings. I calculated the dev cost, mapped the competitive landscape (Otter AI already owned this space), and pushed back. The feature was cut. Keeping the product focused on what actually mattered — field conversations — was more important than a feature that sounded good in a meeting.
02Diagnosed and fixed a 100% activation failure
After launch, nearly every early user was hitting the same wall before they ever experienced the product: a Twilio phone number registration step that required answering a verification call and entering a code — very confusing to users, and easy to abandon. Seed users flagged it immediately. Engineering had no capacity to prioritize it — other product lines came first. Manually guiding each user wasn't scalable either; this was a B2B2C product with end users we'd never directly reach.

I went back to my own Figma designs and stripped them down. Removed the animations I'd built for polish, cut the onboarding steps down to just the verification moment that actually mattered, and replaced motion graphics with static visuals for better device compatibility. The goal: make it impossible to get confused. Two weeks of engineering work became one. Support requests from new users dropped from ~100% to under 10%.
03Built a CRM around conversations — and the data proved it
From day one, the product vision was never "a calling app." It was a CRM that gets smarter with every conversation. Each call or in-person recording feeds an AI-generated client profile — tagged by interests, needs, communication style — that updates automatically. The idea: the more you use it, the more it knows your clients, and the harder it becomes to leave. After launch, the analytics confirmed it. AI summary tab interactions outpaced call events 18× — users were returning to review, reference, and build on past conversations. The product was doing exactly what it was designed to do.
